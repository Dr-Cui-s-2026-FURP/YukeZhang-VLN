# Node 2 Literature Review and Model Selection

## Title

**Literature Review on Vision-Language Navigation for Smart Warehouse AMRs and Justification of the Selected Backbone Direction**

## Literature Review

Vision-language navigation (VLN) studies how an embodied agent can interpret natural-language instructions and use visual observations to move through an environment toward a target. This problem is highly relevant to smart warehouse autonomous mobile robots (AMRs), because human operators often describe goals semantically, for example by referring to shelves, colored boxes, or nearby equipment, rather than by providing exact coordinates. For this reason, VLN offers a promising direction for improving human-robot interaction in warehouse settings. However, the literature also shows that VLN remains difficult because the agent must solve language grounding, visual perception, sequential decision making, and environmental generalization at the same time.

The foundational work in this area is Anderson et al. (2018), which formally defined the VLN task in realistic indoor scenes. Built on Matterport3D, the Room-to-Room (R2R) benchmark pairs natural-language instructions with navigation trajectories in scanned real-world environments. This paper is significant because it shifted navigation away from coordinate-based goal reaching and toward visually grounded instruction following. At the same time, its sequence-to-sequence baseline revealed that VLN was far from solved. The reported success rate in challenging settings remained in the low twenty-percent range, and performance degraded even further in unseen environments. Therefore, Anderson et al. established two enduring research problems: low navigation success and weak generalization to new scenes.

Subsequent work improved model design by replacing simpler recurrent architectures with stronger transformer-based multimodal encoders. A representative example is VLN-BERT (Hong et al., 2021). This model adapts BERT-style cross-modal reasoning to navigation by jointly modeling instructions, panoramic observations, and navigation context. Its most important innovation is the recurrent state token, which allows a transformer-based architecture to maintain step-by-step navigation history. Compared with earlier sequence-to-sequence approaches, VLN-BERT significantly improves grounding between instruction phrases and visible landmarks. Experimentally, it raises performance on unseen R2R environments to roughly the low-sixty-percent range, far above the original benchmark baselines. Even so, the gap between seen and unseen environments remains visible, meaning that better multimodal modeling helps but does not fully solve generalization.

If VLN-BERT demonstrates the value of transformer-based navigation modeling, HAMT (Chen et al., 2021) shows that history representation itself is a central issue. Earlier navigation agents often compressed the past into a fixed-size recurrent hidden state, which could easily lose early information on long trajectories. HAMT addresses this limitation by explicitly modeling the history of panoramic observations and actions as a structured sequence. Its hierarchical transformer design separates view-level, panoramic-level, and temporal-level processing, which preserves both spatial and temporal structure while keeping computation manageable. This is an important conceptual step because it reframes memory not as a minor implementation detail, but as a primary factor affecting long-horizon navigation quality. The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks, including R2R, RxR, R4R, and REVERIE, which confirms that stronger history modeling produces consistent gains across related instruction-following tasks.

Although Anderson et al., VLN-BERT, and HAMT are highly influential, they are still rooted largely in benchmark settings where navigation is simplified by graph structure or other abstractions. A major limitation of benchmark-oriented VLN is that many agents are designed for discrete environments, while real robots operate in continuous spaces with low-level movement constraints. Hong et al. (2022) directly address this issue in *Bridging the Gap Between Learning in Discrete and Continuous Environments for Vision-and-Language Navigation*. Their key insight is that strong discrete VLN agents cannot be directly transferred to continuous control settings because discrete navigation assumes access to a connectivity graph and high-level waypoint actions. To reduce this gap, the paper proposes a candidate waypoint predictor that estimates navigable locations online, thereby allowing agents originally built for high-level action spaces to operate in continuous environments. This contribution is especially relevant to simulation-based robotics projects because it demonstrates how strong benchmark agents can be adapted to more realistic environments without discarding their core architecture.

An even more practical shift appears in LM-Nav (Shah et al., 2022), which moves away from benchmark-first training and toward a modular robotic system built from pre-trained foundation models. Instead of training on language-annotated robot trajectories, LM-Nav combines three pre-trained components: a visual navigation model for execution, a vision-language model for landmark grounding, and a large language model for extracting landmarks from free-form user instructions. The crucial idea is that navigation can be solved by composing complementary models rather than by training a single end-to-end VLN policy on a labeled dataset. This paper is highly relevant to smart warehouse robotics because it uses language as a convenient interface while preserving the robustness of large-scale pre-trained navigation models. It also fits real robotic deployment more naturally than benchmark-only indoor VLN agents, especially when the system designer needs interpretable intermediate outputs such as landmarks, plans, or target descriptions.

More recent work explores whether large language models can contribute explicit reasoning to VLN. NavGPT (Zhou et al., 2024) represents this direction clearly. Instead of treating language models only as parsers or auxiliary modules, NavGPT uses a large language model as the central reasoning engine for zero-shot navigation. At each step, the system takes textual descriptions of the current observation, navigation history, and candidate directions, then reasons about progress, landmarks, sub-goals, and next actions. Importantly, the paper reveals that large language models can provide interpretable planning traces, such as sub-goal decomposition and plan adjustment, which are difficult to access in many conventional learned navigation policies. However, NavGPT also shows that zero-shot reasoning alone is not yet enough to match the performance of trained VLN models. Thus, its strongest contribution is not raw benchmark dominance, but the demonstration that explicit reasoning can make embodied navigation more explainable and potentially more controllable.

In addition to these directly relevant VLN papers, adjacent logistics-oriented work provides useful context but should not be mistaken for a core VLN solution. Zhang et al. (2026), for example, propose an agentic large language model framework for solving complex vehicle routing problems. This paper is not about embodied visual navigation, and it does not address scene grounding or robot movement in the same way as VLN research. Nevertheless, it shows that large language model systems can support planning and decomposition in logistics domains. For the present project, it is best treated as supplementary related work rather than as evidence for a primary backbone choice.

Taken together, the literature suggests a clear development trajectory. First, the field established realistic instruction-following benchmarks and exposed major weaknesses in generalization. Second, transformer-based architectures such as VLN-BERT improved multimodal alignment and sequential modeling. Third, models such as HAMT highlighted the importance of long-horizon memory. Fourth, research began moving beyond benchmark assumptions by adapting agents to continuous environments and by composing modular systems with large pre-trained models, as seen in Hong et al. (2022) and LM-Nav. Finally, newer work such as NavGPT demonstrates the value of explicit high-level reasoning, even when zero-shot performance still lags behind task-specific trained agents.

## Model Selection Justification

Based on this literature, the most defensible backbone direction for this project is **not** to reproduce a benchmark-only discrete VLN agent end to end. Instead, the selected direction should be a **modular language-to-goal navigation pipeline inspired primarily by LM-Nav and supported by the continuous-environment perspective of Hong et al. (2022)**.

This choice is justified for four reasons. First, the project goal is not only to score well on R2R-style benchmarks, but to build a system that can connect natural-language instructions to ROS 2, Nav2, and simulation. Second, warehouse navigation requires semantic interpretation and goal conversion in a deployment-oriented setting, which aligns more naturally with modular decomposition than with a discrete benchmark agent trained to choose graph actions. Third, the continuous-navigation literature shows that direct transfer from discrete VLN to realistic movement is non-trivial, so selecting a backbone that already acknowledges this gap is methodologically stronger. Fourth, the reasoning-oriented direction represented by NavGPT suggests that large language models are especially useful when the system designer wants explainable intermediate outputs, but current evidence still favors using them as part of a structured system rather than as the sole navigation agent.

Therefore, for the later implementation stages of this repository, the recommended backbone is a **modular VLN-inspired architecture** with the following high-level design:

1. A language understanding module that extracts target entities, landmarks, and navigation intent from natural-language instructions.
2. A grounding or mapping layer that converts semantic targets into candidate spatial goals or waypoints.
3. A downstream motion execution layer based on Nav2 in simulation.

In other words, this project should take **LM-Nav as the closest conceptual backbone**, while using insights from VLN-BERT, HAMT, and continuous-environment VLN to guide the design of memory, grounding, and navigation interfaces. This is the most practical and academically defensible choice for a smart warehouse AMR project that must eventually move beyond benchmark settings toward executable simulation-based navigation.
